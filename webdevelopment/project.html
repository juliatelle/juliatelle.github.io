1. Canva

Learning Goal: Create activities and assessments that challenge students to communicate their learning of content while incorporating effective supporting visuals.

Research shows that creating visual explanations better supports students learning than creating verbal ones. Creating visual explanations, such as diagrams, illustrations, flow charts, graphs, and timelines is likely to enhance students’ spatial thinking skills which are “increasingly needed in the contemporary and future world” (Bobek & Tversky, 2016).

Canva is an online graphic design tool that allows students to create high-quality visuals, allowing students to demonstrate their learning in creative and meaningful ways across different content areas:

Language Arts:
  • Summarize research via infographic, communicating evidence through visual means such as charts, graphs, maps, etc.
  • Create posters to showcase independent reading or literary analysis.
Social Studies:
  • Create a timeline of chronological events.
  • Design annotated maps related to historical events.
  • Create informational posters summarizing information about key figures or movements.
Science:
  • Build infographics or diagrams to explain scientific processes.
  • Create graphs, charts, and/or diagrams for visual lab reports.
Math:
  • Create graphs, charts, and/or diagrams for visual lab reports.
  • Design sample graphs, charts, or data tables to test classmates’ understanding.

2. WeVideo

Learning Goal: Design learning activities and assessments via that prompt students to demonstrate their learning through multimedia projects.

A 2019 study found that student-created videos, podcasts, and other digital creations reflect valuable meaning-making processes and contribute to active learning (Anas). There are many stages to preparing the materials, preparing scripts/outlines, selecting needed recording tools and editing the creation to best communicate learning. Podcasts in particular are a valuable tool within the classroom, as they allow for soft-spoken students to use their voice in a safe and controlled space (Prothero, 2023).

WeVideo is an online video-editing site that allows users to create their own multimedia projects, including podcasts. Its diverse features creates many opportunities for student learning and engagement:

Language Arts:
  • Create book trailers or review videos.
  • Outline and perform self-created short stories or plays.
  • Prompt book clubs to create short podcast episodes recapping the most important ideas and events of their reading.
Social Studies:
  • Create biography videos or podcast episodes about important historical figures.
  • Design travel commercials highlighting the geographical and cultural significance of various countries and regions.
  • Synthesize and present research of a historical time period or event.
Science:
  • Record and document the findings of lab experiments.
  • Create short science documentaries explaining key processes and concepts.
  • Outline and present public service announcements related to science-related issues.
Math:
  • Use WeVideo's screencast feature as they solve a math problem, explaining the steps and rationale as they go.
  • Create videos explaining how a math concept applies to real-life (budgeting, GPA calculation, etc.).
  • Record student-led tutorials and “how to” videos, teaching peers a specific math concept or skill.

3. Miro

Learning Goal: Create opportunities for digital discussion and collaboration via online whiteboard tools like Miro.

When designed with clear grading expectations, directions, and expectations for participation, online discussion boards are shown to have several benefits, including developing soft skills, strengthening connections, and practicing active learning (Girolimon, 2024).

Miro is a collaborative online digital whiteboard designed to support group project brainstorming, design, and collaboration. Its interactive features allows for easy ways for students to share their creations with others and for educators to assess learning:

Language Arts:
  • Book clubs can work together to build a visual map of character or theme development over the course of a story.
  • Post brief excerpts of writing, such as thesis statements, to receive peer feedback via digital sticky notes.
Social Studies:
  • Plot events on collaborative timelines or cause and effect maps.
  • Create a hub for debate or research prep, organizing claims and supporting evidence.
Science:
  • Collaboratively build diagrams of scientific concepts, cause and effect maps, or visual representations of lab results.
  • Groups post experimental data, compare results, and identify trends and patterns.
Math:
  • Post incorrectly solved problems. Instruct students to annotate and explain corrections via digital sticky notes.
  • Create collaborative mind maps to show relationships between concepts (ex: fractions, decimals, percentages).
